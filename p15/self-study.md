## B. GRAMMAR SELF-STUDY

### B1. Grammar Summary: Future Perfect, Future Continuous & Speculation Modals

#### 1. Future Perfect (will have + past participle)

**Use:** Actions that will be completed before a specific time in the future.

**Form:**
- Positive: Subject + will have + past participle
- Negative: Subject + will not (won't) have + past participle
- Question: Will + subject + have + past participle?

**Examples:**
- By 2030, most companies **will have adopted** AI technology.
- In five years, quantum computers **will have solved** problems impossible today.
- **Will** blockchain **have revolutionized** finance by 2035?

**Time Expressions:** by 2030/2040/next year, in five years, by the end of this decade, by the time...

---

#### 2. Future Continuous (will be + verb-ing)

**Use:** Actions in progress at a specific time in the future.

**Form:**
- Positive: Subject + will be + verb-ing
- Negative: Subject + will not (won't) be + verb-ing
- Question: Will + subject + be + verb-ing?

**Examples:**
- This time next year, we **will be using** 5G everywhere.
- In 2040, AI **will be assisting** doctors with diagnosis.
- **Will** autonomous vehicles **be transporting** people by 2035?

**Time Expressions:** this time next year, in 2030/2040, ten years from now, in the next decade

---

#### 3. Speculation Modals (Expressing Certainty Levels)

| Certainty Level | Modals | Example |
|-----------------|--------|---------|
| **Very Certain (90-100%)** | will / won't | "AI will transform healthcare." |
| **Moderately Certain (50-80%)** | should / ought to | "This technology should improve efficiency." |
| **Possible (30-70%)** | might / may / could | "Blockchain might replace traditional systems." |
| **Low Probability (10-40%)** | might not / may not | "It might not work as planned." |

#### Model Answer

> **Quantum Computing: Transforming the Future of Problem-Solving**
>
> Quantum computing has emerged as one of the most promising technologies of the 21st century. Over the past decade, researchers have made significant breakthroughs in qubit stability and error correction. By 2040, quantum computers will have revolutionized fields from cryptography to drug discovery, solving problems that are impossible for classical computers today.
>
> Quantum computing has progressed from theoretical concept to practical reality. Companies like IBM and Google have developed quantum processors with increasing numbers of qubits. Research institutions have demonstrated quantum advantage in specific computational tasks. Current applications include optimization problems, molecular simulation for pharmaceutical research, and financial modeling. However, quantum computers still require extremely low temperatures and face challenges with error rates.
>
> By 2035, quantum computers will have become accessible to major corporations and research institutions. Organizations will be using quantum systems to solve complex optimization problems in logistics, finance, and manufacturing. In cryptography, quantum computing will have necessitated the development of new encryption methods, as current systems will be vulnerable to quantum attacks. Ten years from now, pharmaceutical companies will be using quantum simulations to design new drugs in days rather than years. Climate scientists will be running unprecedented weather and climate models. However, full-scale quantum computing might not be available to average users by 2040. The technology could remain specialized and expensive. Some predicted applications may take longer than expected due to technical challenges.
>
> Despite its promise, quantum computing faces significant obstacles. Maintaining qubit coherence remains extremely difficult and expensive. The technology might require decades more development before becoming practical for widespread use. Additionally, the potential to break current encryption systems raises serious security concerns. Nevertheless, quantum computing will likely transform scientific research and problem-solving. Organizations should begin preparing for this quantum future through workforce training and infrastructure planning.
